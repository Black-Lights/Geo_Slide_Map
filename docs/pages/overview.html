Project Overview

Comprehensive Landslide Susceptibility Mapping in Northern Italy

Welcome to our project on landslide susceptibility mapping in Northern Italy. Our mission is to enhance terrain stability awareness and improve risk management through detailed and accurate mapping of landslide-prone areas.

Overview

Introduction

The project focuses on landslide susceptibility mapping in the region of Bergamo, Italy. Landslides are sudden and rapid movements of soil, rock, or debris down a slope. The objective of this project is to identify areas prone to landslides, assess the risk, and support informed decision-making regarding land-use planning, infrastructure development, and mitigation measures.

Objectives

  1. Historical Landslide Data Analysis: Utilize historical landslide data to identify patterns and areas with a higher likelihood of landslides.
  2. Identification of Landslide-Prone Areas: Map the locations and characteristics of past landslides to identify and incorporate susceptible areas into assessments.
  3. Triggering Events Analysis: Understand the relationship between triggering events (e.g., heavy rainfall, earthquakes) and landslide occurrences.
  4. Identification of Landslide Types: Map different types of landslides to refine susceptibility assessments.
  5. Validation and Calibration: Use historical landslides to validate and calibrate the models.
  6. Land-Use Planning and Risk Management: Integrate landslide data into planning and risk management to mitigate potential impacts.

Area of Interest

The study area in Bergamo, Italy, is chosen due to its mountainous terrain, historical landslide events, high population density, and infrastructure vulnerability. The analysis of susceptibility factors will help in identifying low-risk areas, prioritizing mitigation measures, and reducing the impact of landslides on lives, infrastructure, and sustainable development.

Methodology

The methodology involves using various geospatial data layers, such as slope, roads, rivers, population, NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index), faults, land use and land cover, Digital Terrain Model (DTM), and aspect. The process includes:

  1. Data Collection and Preprocessing: Collecting layers including DTM, DUSAF, roads, rivers, faults, landslide inventory, NDVI, and the shapefile for the area of interest. Preprocessing includes clipping, reprojecting layers in the same CRS, and grouping.
  2. Data Analysis: Analyzing each layer to produce maps such as aspect, slope, no landslide zones, and landslide zones.
  3. Classification: Creating training and testing sample points for classification.
  4. Modeling: Developing landslide susceptibility maps using various modeling techniques.
  5. Accuracy Assessment: Calculating accuracy metrics to evaluate the reliability of the susceptibility maps.

Data Layers and Their Impact

  1. Aspect: Refers to the compass direction that a slope faces. Influences landslide susceptibility by affecting moisture distribution, sun exposure, vegetation patterns, snow accumulation, and erosion patterns.
  2. DTM (Digital Terrain Model): Provides detailed information on slope gradients, elevation changes, and topographic features that influence slope stability.
  3. DUSAF (Land Use and Land Cover): Influences landslide susceptibility through vegetation cover, soil erosion, urbanization, and water management practices.
  4. Faults: Geological fractures in the Earth's crust create zones of weakness and potential failure along their boundaries.
  5. NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index): Assesses vegetation health and density, providing insights into slope stability and erosion control.
  6. Planimetric Curves: Represent slope contours or elevation lines on a 2D map, identifying areas with potentially higher susceptibility to landslides.
  7. Population: Mapping the presence and distribution of people in areas prone to landslides highlights the potential impact on human lives and infrastructure.

Output

The output includes a landslide susceptibility map depicting the spatial distribution of landslide risk in the region. The map ranges from low to high susceptibility based on the integrated analysis of multiple layers. Additionally, an assessment of the accuracy of the landslide map ensures its reliability for decision-making. The results and layers can be visualized through a WebGIS platform, allowing stakeholders to interact with the data and support effective landslide risk management.
